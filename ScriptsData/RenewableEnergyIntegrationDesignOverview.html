Overview

This repository provides design solutions that aid the operation of power systems with high penetration of renewable energy sources. The developed models comply with current IEEE/IEC and national grid standards.
The traditional power system is experiencing a shift in generation due to the increase in penetration of inverter-based renewable resources (IBRs). Renewable sources, including solar and wind, are intermittent and increase the variability in power system operation. To manage the variability introduced by inverter based renewables, the flexibility in an existing power system should be increase through energy storage, network reinforcement by flexible AC transmission systems (FACTS) and high voltage DC transmission (HVDC), and demand management. To determine system strength, inertia requirements, design control, and protection systems, you must perform time domain modeling and simulation of IBR interfaced power systems.
Key words: Grid forming(GFM) battery energy storage systems in PV plant, references

Challenges with Renewable Integration

The increase in renewable energy penetration in power system has replaced conventional synchronous machine sources with IBRs. An IBR-dominated power system has reduced synchronization torque, inertia, and limited short circuit current [1]. These systems exhibit significant changes in voltage and frequency with intermittent changes in injected renewable power. This figure shows an anology to describe this situation [2].
Most grid-connected renewables operate with grid-following (GFL) control, which relies on fast synchronization with the grid to control its active and reactive currents [2]. In an IBR-dominated grid, these inverters might fail to track the grid voltage and maintain synchronism, thus leading to instability.
To manage the variability of renewable power, the system operator focuses on options for increasing flexibility across the power system. The power system flexibility can be increased through battery energy storage, grid forming controls, demand side management, network reinforcement through flexible AC transmission systems (FACTS) and high voltage DC transmission (HVDC). These methodologies can help in improving the stability and reliability of a power system with high IBR penetration.

Assess Stability of Inverter-Based Resources (IBRs) using Admittance Scan Technique

This study evaluates the capabilities of the admittance scan block in detecting poorly damped and unstable modes in a power systems with high renewable penetration.
Inverters have fast-acting controllers and limited short-circuit capacity [1]. IBRs replace conventional synchronous generators, reducing grid strength, which makes it challenging to maintain a stable system.
Interactions between the IBR control and the grid cause oscillations in the three-phase voltages and currents, which can cause the protection system to trip the renewable plant.
Admittance scanning is a data driven method of stability assessment, which uses only measurements made at the POI of the IBR, to determine the frequency vs. admittance characteristics of the grid-connected system. These characteristics are valid for the operating condition over which you estimate the admittance
